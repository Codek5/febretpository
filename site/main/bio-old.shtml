About Me



Today, most research involves the analysis of scientific phenomena of ever-increasing scale and complexity, and requires the concentrated effort of interdisciplinary teams: scientists from different backgrounds whose work involves large, heterogeneous data sources. As the scale and complexity of data continue to grow, large scale visualization instruments like display walls and immersive environments become increasingly essential to researchers. In particular, immersive systems are an attractive option for exploring 3D spatial data such as molecules, astrophysical phenomena, and geoscience datasets. On the other hand, display walls with their high resolution help interpret large datasets, offering both overview and resolution, or can be used to lay out a variety of correlated visual data and documents for collaborative analysis.

The main goal of my research is to create novel tools that scientists can leverage in their quest to turn raw data into discovery. To reach this goal, I believe it is fundamental to interpret current technological trends, and understand how they could facilitate the work and interaction dynamics of a research team. For instance, advancements in display technology led to the production of larger, affordable thin-bezel LCD monitors with built-in support for stereoscopic 3D. These advancements made it conceivable to merge the best-in-class capabilities of immersive Virtual Reality systems with the best-in-class capabilities of Ultra-high-resolution Display Environments thus creating conceptually new immersive environments which we refer to as Hybrid Reality Environments (HREs), such as the CAVE2 system. These fully digital ‘war rooms’ can support the simultaneous work of multiple users and can work both as immersive environments and classic large displays. Furthermore, we observed an explosion in the availability of powerful, portable devices that represent a platform to both interact with a larger system and enrich the capabilities of such system as an additional display device.

These hybrid multi-display and multi-user environments have been envisioned in the past, but they are now reaching their full technological feasibility. If we want to make full use of the capabilities of these systems, we need to understand how users want to work and interact with them; we also need to provide developers with a consistent software infrastructure that can be used as a platform for applications on such systems.

I am addressing these challenges in my current research: I collaborated with multidisciplinary teams interacting with large displays, and worked towards a conceptual foundation and software infrastructure for co-located team work in hybrid immersive environments. In particular, I observed how large display users need access to a system that lets them easily and seamlessly manage display space and content: co-located collaboration often entails multiple phases that alternate full group work with individuals or sub-groups working in parallel: moreover the work may focus on a single issue, on different views into that single issue, or on independent issues: the ability to easily re-configure the display space is fundamental to support such heterogeneous tasks.

I envisioned the need for a convergence in software design for display walls and immersive environments, proposing the integration of display wall software, an immersive environment framework and additional components into an "operating system" for Hybrid Reality Environments. This operating system aims to solve two major challenges with alternative approaches:

  • Static allocation of 3D and 2D views: although an HRE is capable of displaying 2D and 3D content at the same time, software relies on static configurations describing how the physical display space should be split into 2D and 3D views. Multiple predefined configurations give the end users some flexibility, but require restarting and resetting all running applications.
  • Lack of unified interaction: the 2D and 3D portions on an HRE often rely on inconsistent interaction schemes: for instance Pointing semantics may use absolute movement on the 3D half and relative movement on the 2D half. Physically separate interaction devices may be required. Or, when a single device is used, interaction may lead to conflicting results (i.e. navigating a 3D view moves 2D views).

The remainder of my Ph.D. will be dedicated to completing the implementation of the HRE operating system, working in particular on managing the interaction between multiple users and multiple applications running on the system. I plan to carry on a user study to demonstrate the effectiveness of hybrid visualization and dynamic display configuration on a scientific visual analysis task, and to further characterize the interaction patterns of a small group of users with an HRE.

As a future research direction, I would like to further explore the use of hybrid environments for collaborative visualization, both local and remote. I see two main paths of advancement: HRE-to-HRE collaboration and cross-device, or asymmetric, collaboration. The HRE-to-HRE scenario would involve two remote teams working inside separate Hybrid Reality Environments, sharing workspaces, views and applications between them. This work could make use of the existing results in shared virtual environments, telepresence and remote collaboration with display walls, but would need to merge concepts from all three into a novel unified model. Cross-device collaboration is exemplified by a team of users within an HRE, collaborating with one or more remote users on mobile devices (i.e. tablets). In this scenario, collaboration is asymmetric since the interaction and visualization capabilities are radically different between the two user groups. A possible outcome of this research would be a conceptual model for dynamically mapping views, workspaces and interactions between the HRE and the mobile devices, in order to maximize the collaboration bandwidth between the user groups. I believe large high resolution displays are destined to become ubiquitous. Portable displays already are. Making them part of a single ecosystem for scientific visualization and collaboration can be a force multiplier for scientific discovery, enabling researchers to better engage with their data and, more importantly, with each other.
